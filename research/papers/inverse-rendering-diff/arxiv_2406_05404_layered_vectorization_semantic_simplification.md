relevance: Direct analog of mokuhanga staged construction — they iteratively simplify the target image via Score Distillation, then vectorize each simplified residual as a NEW layer. Each round captures coarser structure first, finer detail later. This is the staged hierarchical solver chuck-mcp wants but implemented in vector graphics.

Algorithmic moves to import:
- Build a hierarchy by REPEATEDLY simplifying the target (semantic-aware), not by re-running on the same target.
- Each layer optimizes against a different simplified residual, so early layers are forced to capture broad structure and later layers cannot mimic them.
- This is the principled answer to chuck-mcp's question: "how do we force batch 1 to take large supports without re-litigating in batch 3?" Answer: batch 1 fits a coarsely-simplified target; batch 3 fits the original.

Early work relied on segmentating images into non-overlapping 2D patches, such as triangular\n[\n7\n,\n3\n,\n49\n]\n, or other irregular shapes\n[\n30\n]\n. Later research focused on refining the boundaries of these decompositions, introducing curved boundaries to better capture curvilinear features\n[\n55\n,\n59\n,\n34\n]\nand gradients\n[\n48\n]\n.\nAnother approach to image vectorization tackles it as a curve or polygon fitting problem. This includes methods like diffusion curves, which represent images at the extrema of the gradient1 and then render them through the Poisson equation\n[\n37\n,\n56\n,\n66\n]\n. Other vectorization techniques are particularly suited for non-photorealistic images, such as clip art\n[\n8\n,\n23\n,\n15\n,\n11\n]\n, line drawings\n[\n14\n,\n58\n]\n, cartoons\n[\n64\n]\n, gray-scale manga\n[\n47\n]\n, and pixel art\n[\n28\n]\n.\nThe advent of deep learning has led researchers to approach vectorization through neural networks. A key enabler is the development of differentiable rasterizers\n[\n32\n]\n, which bridge the vector and raster domains. Im2Vec\n[\n41\n]\nemploys a variational auto-encoder (VAE) on Fonts and Emoji datasets, to map the input image to a latent space and generate a similar vector. ClipGen\n[\n43\n]\ntrains an LSTM on clipart vectors of ten categories to optimize vector primitives. Other network-based vectorization methods focus on line-drawing images\n[\n13\n]\n. Chen et al.\n[\n5\n]\nleverage a transformer model to assemble vectorizations from simple primitives. Some approaches avoid model learning. LIVE\n[\n35\n]\nprogressively optimize closed cubic B\u00e9zier curves to the large difference regions between the rendered SVG and target image. SAMVG\n[\n68\n]\ninitializes primitives based on segmented masks from the target raster image. SuperSVG\n[\n25\n]\ntrains a model to predict vectors from the superpixel-based segmentation of images. SGLIVE\n[\n67\n]\nextends the capability of LIVE to support radial gradients via a gradient-aware segmentation. Chen et al.\n[\n6\n]\nencapsulate texture in the vector optimization. Hirschorn et al.\n[\n20\n]\nintroduce an iterative process that adds primitives based on pixel clustering, and removes primitives with low-ranking scores after optimization.\nOur vectorization method is also model-free, leveraging differential rendering. Unlike prior works\n[\n35\n,\n20\n,\n68\n,\n67\n]\nthat optimize towards a single target image, our method generates a sequence of progressively simplified intermediate images as optimization targets. This sequence effectively captures its underlying topology structure from coarse to fine, guiding vectors toward a hierarchical representation of objects and their topologies.\nLayer Decomposition\nLayers are an efficient structure for image manipulation and editing\n[\n4\n]\n. More recently, deep models have been trained to decompose images into transparent layers\n[\n60\n]\n. However, those layers are RGBA semi-transparent layers in raster image format.\nAnother bunch of works decomposes images into vectorized layers. Several work vectorize an image into shapes with linear color gradient\n[\n15\n,\n42\n,\n11\n]\n, based on the\nalpha compositing\n[\n39\n]\n. Those methods mainly deal with clip art images, which may become prone to errors when applied to natural images. Other work decomposes an image into sequences of brushstrokes to recover the step-by-step painting process\n[\n69\n,\n46\n]\n. Taking the target image as input, our process begins with\nProgressive Image Simplification\nthat employs the generative diffusion model based on score distillation sampling\n[\n38\n]\n, to generate a sequence of simplified images,\nreferred to as the\nsimplified image sequence\n. Taking this simplified image sequence as guidance, our method goes through a two-stage vector reconstruction.\nThe first stage involves constructing structure-wise vectors that capture both implicit and explicit semantic structures. To begin, semantic segmentation\n[\n27\n]\nis applied to each image in the simplified sequence, to extract pixels of semantic areas, referred to as\nmasks\n, at various levels of detail. Those segmented masks are sorted and organized into back-to-front layers based on their overlap relationships. Using the raw boundaries of segmented masks as structure-wise vectors can be intricate; therefore, optimizing structure-wise vectors is employed. Specifically, structure-wise vectors are initialized per mask and optimized via differential rendering\n[\n32\n]\nwith the\nlayer-wise structure loss\n, which measures the shape alignment between structure-wise vectors and their corresponding masks, to ensure the vector representation accurately maintains semantic structures.\nThe second stage involves visual refinement. Color fitting is first performed on the structure-wise vectors, serving as the visual basis and frozen. Then taking the rasterized image of frozen structure-wise vectors, we compute its visual differences to the target image. Then visual-wise vector primitives are initialized to regions with large visual differences and optimized toward the target image to minimize the\nvisual fidelity loss\n. Below, we introduce the key components and loss functions.\n5.1\nStage I: Structural Construction\nLayering of Segmented Masks\nThe sequence of simplified images is semantically segmented to detect object masks. Subsequently, the masks are organized into layers, with large masks on the back layer and small masks overlaid on the front. Masks within a layer are not intersected with each other. As illustrated in Figure\n8\n, masks are added progressively from the most simplified image to the least simplified (i.e., the input target), with each mask placed in the furthest back layer where it does not overlap with other masks already positioned on the same layer.\nFigure 8\n:\nMask layering: iterating from the most simplified to the least, a mask is only added into the layer from back to front, provided it does not intersect with other masks already in that layer.\nOnce the masks are layered, each mask\u2019s boundary is\ntraced and simplified using the Douglas\u2013Peucker algorithm\n[\n9\n]\n) to reduce the number of points in the boundary.
