## Shiramine Paper: Technical Specifications

Shiramine is produced by the Awagami Factory in Tokushima, Japan. It is one of the standard washi papers used in professional mokuhanga editioning. Two grades are commonly available:

**Standard Shiramine (machine-made)**:
- Composition: 30% Thai Kozo, 70% SP (softwood) pulp
- Weight: 110 g/m²
- Sizing: Internal sizing (in-sheet, not surface-applied)
- Surface: Natural white, slightly textured

**Shiramine Select (editioning grade)**:
- Composition: 30% Kozo, 55% alpha cellulose, 15% hemp
- Weight: 110 g/m²
- Sizing: Internal (alum-rosin or similar)
- Surface: More uniform surface, suitable for fine art registration

The Chuck Close "Emma" (2002) edition documentation specifies "Shiramine paper" without further grade designation — likely Shiramine Select given the production context.

## Why Shiramine for a 132-Pass Edition

For an edition involving 132 individual wet impressions per sheet, the paper must maintain dimensional stability across repeated dampening and drying cycles. The key properties that make Shiramine viable for this:

1. **Internal sizing**: Reduces the rate of water absorption into the fiber. The pigment-nori mixture sits at or near the surface for adequate transfer time before being absorbed. Without sizing, rapid absorption prevents clean pigment transfer and causes bleed between adjacent color zones.

2. **Kozo fiber**: Long-fibered Kozo (paper mulberry) provides tensile strength when wet. Short-fiber papers (wood pulp) distort or tear under repeated wet impression; Kozo maintains dimensional integrity. The long fibers interlock to resist stretching.

3. **Weight at 110 gsm**: Heavier than traditional ukiyo-e papers (~35–50 gsm), which aids stability. Traditional ukiyo-e used thinner, more absorbent papers that required precise baren technique; the heavier weight allows more forgiving pressure variation.

4. **Alpha cellulose component**: The 55% alpha cellulose in Select grade provides archival stability and surface consistency — important for editions intended for fine art collectors who expect permanence.

## Paper Conditioning Protocol

For multi-pass mokuhanga at edition scale, paper conditioning before each printing session is standard practice. Sheets are lightly misted or damp-packed (sandwiched between damp newsprint, wrapped in foil overnight) to achieve uniform moisture throughout the fiber. Inconsistent moisture produces uneven registration as dry sections of the sheet contract while wet sections expand.

For a 132-pass edition, the conditioning protocol is likely structured across multiple days: batch-dampening groups of sheets, printing a sequence of passes while the moisture profile is consistent, then allowing sheets to partially dry before the next batch.
