## The Contemporary Mokuhanga Revival

Mokuhanga is experiencing a global revival since approximately 2000, driven by several factors: the non-toxic, water-based nature of the technique (contrasted with acid-etching and oil-based lithography), the increasing internationalization of printmaking education, and the prestige attached to fine art editions produced by Pace Editions. The IMC conferences beginning in 2009 formalized the international practitioner community.

Key indicators: The Mokuhanga Project Space, Mokuhanga Sisters, and The Unfinished Print podcast all represent practitioner-led documentation infrastructure that did not exist before 2010. The Pigment Tokyo online store, offering mokuhanga-specific pigment supplies internationally, opened in 2015 and has produced detailed technical blog content (including the dosa sizing concentration article referenced in this research).

## Shibata's Dual Role

Shibata occupies an unusual position in this revival: he is simultaneously a master printer for blue-chip fine art publishers (Pace Editions, Aspinwall Editions) and an individual artist who teaches (Cooper Union) and exhibits his own reduction woodcut work. His position as a conduit between traditional Japanese technique and contemporary Western fine art contexts makes him an unusually important figure for understanding how mokuhanga functions at production scale.

The absence of detailed written documentation from Shibata himself (no blog, no podcast appearance in results, no essay-length process document found) is notable and represents a gap in publicly available scholarship. His process knowledge exists primarily in his printed work and in the Sultan catalog interviews.

## Practitioners Extending the Technique

The contemporary mokuhanga community has produced documented innovations relevant to chuck-mcp:
- **Mike Lyon**: CNC-generated blocks from photographic luminosity separation, demonstrating digital-to-traditional workflow
- **April Vollmer**: Multi-block techniques for Western artists, documentation of complex layer interactions
- **Takuji Hamanaka**: Demonstrations at Brooklyn Print Fair showing multiple colors from a single block per pass
- **Tanuki Prints (David Bull)**: Detailed blog documentation of bokashi, nori, and carving for traditional ukiyo-e at edition scale

The community consensus on production-scale mokuhanga aligns with what the Shibata-Close documentation shows: blocks are form libraries, not color carriers; pass count drives color complexity; light-to-dark sequencing is mechanically enforced; and paper conditioning is as important as ink preparation.
