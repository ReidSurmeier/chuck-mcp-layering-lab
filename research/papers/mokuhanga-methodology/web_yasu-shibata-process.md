## Biographical Baseline

Yasuyuki (Yasu) Shibata was born in 1968 in Osaka, Japan. He earned his BFA from Kyoto Seika University, where he studied printmaking under Professor Akira Kurosaki — a major figure in modern mokuhanga known for intense color and prints requiring up to 70 passes. Shibata moved to the United States in 1991 and began his career at Tyler Graphics, working on editions for Frank Stella, David Salle, and Helen Frankenthaler. He subsequently joined Pace Editions in New York, where he has worked as master printer exclusively in mokuhanga. He also teaches relief printing at Cooper Union.

His work resides in the permanent collections of the New York Public Library, Cleveland Museum of Art, and Smith College Museum of Art.

## Dual Technique: Ukiyo-e Multi-Block + Reduction

The single most important technical fact about Shibata's practice is that he combines two structurally distinct woodblock methods: (1) the traditional ukiyo-e multi-block approach, in which separately carved blocks each contribute distinct color areas, and (2) reduction woodcut technique, in which a single block is progressively carved away and reprinted between each pass. He does not default to pure multi-block: he fuses both systems within a single edition.

In his own artwork, works are described as "reduction woodcuts mounted on western paper," demonstrating that the reduction approach governs his personal aesthetic. For collaborative editions (Close, Wood, Siena), the balance shifts toward multi-block with reduction elements. This hybrid is what makes 27 blocks produce 132 passes without a 1:1 mapping.

## Collaboration Process

Shibata's documented workflow for collaborative editions involves: making a tracing from the artist's original painting or drawing; performing color separations onto multiple blocks; carving the blocks with traditional hand tools; mixing colors from dry pigments for each block; and producing test proofs for the artist's review. The process iterates — proof, adjustment, re-proof — until the artist approves the color fidelity. Only then is the edition run.

For Jonas Wood's "Four Landscapes" (2021, edition of 35), the process spanned over two years and involved 12–20 blocks per print inked with up to 73 individual colors — confirming that Shibata's multi-block strategy involves substantial re-inking of single blocks with different pigment mixes per pass.

## Training Lineage

Shibata's teacher, Akira Kurosaki (1937–2019), was known for prints using up to 8 blocks, 15 colors, and 70 passes. Kurosaki's own practice established the precedent that pass count greatly exceeds block count — through re-inking the same block with different pigment loads. Shibata scales this substantially further: Kurosaki's 70-pass ceiling becomes Shibata's baseline for complex collaborative work.

## Pigment and Paper Practice (Inferred)

Based on training lineage (Kurosaki used hanji — Korean mulberry paper), corroborated by Shibata's own works specifying "Kizuki-shi paper" and "Kozo paper" as materials, Shibata works with high-quality Japanese washi. For Close's Emma, the specified substrate is Shiramine (110 gsm internally-sized Kozo/alpha cellulose/hemp blend), likely selected for its dimensional stability across 132 wet impressions. Pigments used are consistent with contemporary mokuhanga practice: water-based, mixed with rice-starch paste (nori) and water. Shibata does not use oil-based inks.
