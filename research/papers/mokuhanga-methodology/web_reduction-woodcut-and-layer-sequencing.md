## Reduction Woodcut: Core Mechanism

In reduction woodcut, a single block is printed multiple times. After each color pass, more material is carved away from the block, permanently altering it. The next pass prints through whatever surface remains. This produces effects that are impossible in pure multi-block work: colors blend spatially through successive carving, and the final edition count is strictly limited because the original block state is destroyed.

Shibata uses reduction technique in his own work (as documented in exhibition materials describing his prints as "reduction woodcuts mounted on western paper"). The critical implication for his collaborative work with Close is that some of the 132 passes in Emma may involve progressive carving of blocks between passes — not just re-inking. If true, this means the chuck-mcp planner cannot treat the block as a static spatial mask. Some blocks evolve spatially during the edition run.

## Light-to-Dark Sequencing: The Iron Rule

All documented mokuhanga traditions agree on light-to-dark pass sequencing. The physical reasons:

1. **Pigment physics**: Light pigments (whites, pale yellows) have low tinting strength and are overwhelmed by any underlying dark layer. They must be printed onto clean (or near-clean) paper to read as light.

2. **Moisture conditioning**: The first passes onto dry paper require more paper pre-dampening. Subsequent passes benefit from moisture residue in the paper fiber from earlier passes — reducing overall prep time and improving consistency.

3. **Offset prevention**: Printing from light to dark reduces the risk of dark pigment from a block being picked up (offset) by the still-wet surface of the printed sheet and contaminating a later pass from the same or different block.

4. **Ink contamination on block**: Re-inking a block with a lighter color after a dark color requires thorough cleaning; doing the reverse (dark after light) is less problematic.

## The Controversial Exception: Key Block Timing

In traditional ukiyo-e, the sumi key block (outline block, black ink) is sometimes printed last, after all color blocks. The rationale: sumi applied first would be obscured by subsequent color passes; applied last it sits on top of all color as a crisp black line. Salter documents this in traditional practice.

However, contemporary artists who want color without outline (as in Close's work — no key block in Emma, which uses color juxtaposition to define form rather than drawn outlines) avoid this question entirely. Emma has no sumi key block.

The exception matters for chuck-mcp if any "key tone" or "dark anchor" pass is planned: it should typically be scheduled late in the sequence, not early.

## Multi-Block vs. Reduction: The Shibata Hybrid

Shibata's specific innovation, based on available documentation, is combining the two approaches:
- **Multi-block** for the primary spatial structure: different forms are on different blocks, preventing contamination between adjacent color zones
- **Reduction elements** within individual blocks: the same block may be progressively modified between certain passes to evolve its spatial footprint

This hybrid allows him to achieve close-tolerance registration (because the block structure provides stable spatial reference) while also enabling spatial complexity beyond what simple multi-block allows (because some blocks change spatially mid-edition).

For a 27-block, 132-pass system producing 113 colors: multi-block alone would require 113 blocks (one per color); pure reduction alone would require only 1–3 blocks. The 27-block hybrid sits between these extremes, using form-library-style block reuse with selective reduction for spatial evolution.

## Sequencing Rule Summary

From all sources combined, the operative sequencing rules for production-scale mokuhanga:
1. Light/transparent washes come first — they set tonal key and condition paper
2. Broad flat tones come second — establish color masses across major areas
3. Bokashi gradations come third — model form and spatial depth within established areas
4. Saturated mid-tones and mixed hues come fourth — the primary color-building zone
5. Dark, opaque, or detail passes come last — including any sumi-type anchoring
6. Reduction passes (if any) are interleaved with their related block's passes, not batched separately
