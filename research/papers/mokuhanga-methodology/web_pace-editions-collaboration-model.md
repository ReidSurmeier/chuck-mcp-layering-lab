## The Pace Editions Production Model

Pace Editions (now Pace Prints) operates as a fine-art print publisher employing master printers who hold deep technical expertise in specific media. Shibata is the studio's dedicated mokuhanga specialist. The model is not "artist directs, printer executes" — it is closer to genuine co-authorship: the printer's technical vocabulary shapes what is possible and proposes solutions the artist could not anticipate.

## Documented Production Figures Across Multiple Collaborations

Three works provide quantitative benchmarks for the Shibata production scale:

| Work | Year | Blocks | Colors | Passes | Duration |
|------|------|--------|--------|--------|----------|
| Emma (Close) | 2002 | 27 | 113 | 132 | ~2 years |
| Self-Portrait (Close) | 2015 | 24 | 84 | — | ~3 years |
| Four Landscapes (Wood) | 2021 | 12–20 | 73 | — | ~2 years |

The trajectory from Emma to Self-Portrait shows Close-Shibata optimizing: fewer blocks (27→24), fewer colors (113→84), similar production time. The Jonas Wood project with 12–20 blocks and 73 colors is proportionally simpler, suggesting the 2-year timeline is partly iteration time (artist approval loops) rather than purely carving and printing duration.

## The "Multiple Colors Per Block Per Pass" Structural Rule

Across all three projects, colors exceed blocks by a factor of 3–6×. This is the defining structural property of Shibata's system: a single carved block is loaded with different pigments and reprinted. The carving is stable; the ink configuration varies. This means:
- Block count defines the library of spatial forms available
- Pass count defines how many times each spatial form is used (with different color)
- Colors = unique (block × ink configuration) combinations across all passes

The chuck-mcp planner's current batch structure (4 broad-tone + 4 mid + detail) implicitly treats blocks as color carriers rather than form carriers. Shibata's system inverts this: blocks are form libraries, colors are deployment decisions.

## Collaborative Approval Loop

From the documented workflow: after carving is complete and initial proofs are pulled, the artist and printer engage in an approval loop — progressive proofs are reviewed, color adjustments made (re-inking the same block with adjusted mixture, or revising the carving), and trial passes pulled until the artist approves. Only after full approval does the full edition run. This implies the 132-pass sequence is not designed entirely in advance — it is partially emergent from the proofing conversation.

## Progressive Proof Display

The 2003-2004 "Chuck Close Prints: Process and Collaboration" exhibition displayed progressive proofs for Emma alongside the final print and actual woodblocks. The progressive proofs showed each state of the cumulative image as blocks were added in sequence. The exhibition catalog (Sultan/Shiff, Princeton 2003) contains interviews with Close and his master printers and constitutes the primary written documentation of the methodological thinking.
