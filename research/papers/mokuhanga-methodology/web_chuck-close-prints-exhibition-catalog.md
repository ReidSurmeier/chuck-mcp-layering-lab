## Exhibition Overview

"Chuck Close Prints: Process and Collaboration" was organized by Terrie Sultan and the Blaffer Gallery, Art Museum of the University of Houston. It toured to multiple venues including:
- The Modern Art Museum of Fort Worth
- The Metropolitan Museum of Art, New York (2004)
- The Mint Museum, Charlotte
- Madison Museum of Contemporary Art

The exhibition focused on Close's printmaking as process documentation — the creative method is the subject as much as the finished work.

## Exhibition Content Directly Relevant to Emma

The Met's press release and venue documentation confirm:
- Progressive and state proofs were displayed alongside editioned works
- Actual woodblocks and etching plates were included in the exhibition
- The display was designed to "visualize the artist's creative processes"
- Emma (2002) was featured as the pinnacle example of his woodblock work

Progressive proofs for Emma would show the cumulative state of the image after each sequential block addition — providing a visual map of the layer sequence. These proofs are the closest available documentation of Shibata's actual sequencing logic.

## Chuck Close on Collaborative Printmaking

Close's documented statements from the exhibition context: "Like any corporation, I have the benefit of the brainpower of everyone who is working for me. My prints have been truly collaborative, even though control is something that I give up reluctantly."

This statement establishes the essential dynamic: Close provides color palette and mark character (from his painting); Shibata provides the technical translation into the block-pass system. The "reluctant control" framing suggests Close was actively involved in proofing decisions — the iteration count likely driven by his demanding color accuracy requirements.

## Close's Printmaking Range

The exhibition documented Close's complete print practice across media: mezzotint, etching, aquatint, lithography, silkscreen, linoleum cut, handmade paper, Japanese and European woodcut. Emma is the culmination of his woodblock work in terms of technical complexity. The 2015 Self-Portrait (24 blocks, 84 colors, Karl Hecksher as printer) continues the series but with slightly reduced complexity — possibly representing learning from Emma's two-year production.
