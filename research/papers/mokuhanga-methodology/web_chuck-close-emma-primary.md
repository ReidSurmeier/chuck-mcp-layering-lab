## Object Record

"Emma" (2002) is a woodcut in 113 colors on Shiramine paper, published by Pace Editions Inc., New York. Dimensions: 43 × 35 inches (109.2 × 88.9 cm). Edition of 55 plus 8 artist's proofs (confirmed from auction records). Subject: Close's infant niece Emma, depicted as a gridded abstraction of colored loops, dots, and lozenges — Close's signature late-period mosaic style applied to the ukiyo-e medium.

## Production Scale

The canonical production figures, corroborated across Pace Editions, gallery listings, and the University of Houston process documentation:
- 27 woodblocks carved
- 113 distinct colors achieved
- 132 individual printing passes per impression
- Approximately 18–24 months of production time (sources vary between "over eighteen months" and "two years")

Master printer Yasu Shibata executed all carving and printing using traditional mokuhanga (Japanese water-based woodblock) technique. Close spent approximately three months on the underlying painting; Shibata's production phase dominated the timeline.

## The 27/113/132 Relationship

The ratio of 27 blocks to 113 colors to 132 passes reveals the core structural fact about Shibata's approach: individual woodblocks are printed multiple times, often with different pigment mixtures on each pass. A single carved block is not a single color — it is a matrix that receives different ink configurations across passes. This means the average block produced approximately 4–5 distinct color impressions, and many blocks were reloaded and reprinted sequentially. This is the critical fact that challenges the simpler "1 block = 1 color" mental model.

## Shiramine Paper

The print was produced on Shiramine paper, a machine-made washi from the Awagami Factory in Tokushima, Japan. Shiramine Select (the editioning grade) is composed of 30% Kozo, 55% alpha cellulose, and 15% hemp at 110 g/m². It is internally sized, relatively heavyweight for washi, and designed to accept repeated wet impression without dimensional distortion. Its internal sizing distinguishes it from unsized or surface-sized washi, reducing bleed between layers without requiring the printer to apply additional dosa (alum-gelatin sizing) before use.

## Process and Collaboration Exhibition Context

The print appeared in the exhibition "Chuck Close Prints: Process and Collaboration" (2003–2004, organized by Blaffer Gallery, Houston; toured to the Met and elsewhere). The exhibition displayed progressive proofs alongside the editioned prints, and the accompanying catalog (Sultan and Shiff, Princeton University Press, 2003) contains interviews with Close and his master printers discussing the collaborative methodology. The progressive proof sequence for Emma shows cumulative block additions — each state adding spatial depth to Close's mosaic.
