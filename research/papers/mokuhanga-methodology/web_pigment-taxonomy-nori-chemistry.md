## Traditional Pigment Taxonomy

Mokuhanga historically used three pigment categories:

**Mineral pigments (ganryo)**: Ground natural minerals. Key historical examples include bengara (iron oxide red/brown), ultramarine (from lapis lazuli before synthetic production), malachite green, azurite blue, and shell white (gofun from ground oyster shell). Mineral pigments are typically opaque, have high lightfastness, and require substantial grinding (45min for 1 tablespoon using the traditional wooden pestle on a cherry board). They resist flowing in water — nori paste is essential to keep them in suspension during printing.

**Organic/lake pigments (senshoku-ryo)**: Natural dye-based pigments precipitated onto an inert substrate. Historical examples include safflower red (beni), indigo (ai), gamboge, and various plant-derived dyes. Lakes are typically transparent, fugitive to light, and produce the rich transluency characteristic of traditional Japanese prints. Modern synthetic lake pigments (Naphthol reds, Hansa yellows, Phthalo blues/greens) replace most natural lakes in contemporary practice while preserving the translucent optical behavior.

**Carbon black (sumi ink)**: A separate category — soot-based carbon in animal glue suspension. Sumi is used for the key block (outline block) in traditional ukiyo-e. Contemporary artists frequently skip the sumi key block and instead build from light color passes, using color gradation to imply line.

## Contemporary Synthetic Pigment Families in Mokuhanga

Contemporary practitioners (McClain's Printmaking Supplies, Pigment Tokyo, Holbein) use:
- **Naphthol reds** (PR112, PR188): Bright warm reds-to-oranges, semi-transparent, excellent mokuhanga performance
- **Hansa yellows** (PY3, PY74, PY97): Clean monoazo yellows; transparent grade is highly valued for glazing passes
- **Phthalo blues and greens** (PB15, PG7, PG36): High tinting strength, transparent, can be used very dilute for atmospheric passes
- **Quinacridone** (PR122, PV19): Transparent violets-to-pinks, high lightfastness
- **Iron oxide series** (PY42, PR101, PBr7): Opaque earth tones corresponding to traditional bengara family
- **Titanium white** (PW6): Opaque, used sparingly; creates opacity in mixed colors

Holbein Pigment Pastes (widely used in contemporary mokuhanga including by Japanese practitioners) contain 30–70% pure pigment by weight in a water/wetting agent/dispersant base, pre-ground to printing consistency, eliminating the traditional grinding step.

## Nori Paste: Function and Concentration

Rice-starch paste (nori) serves two functions: it keeps pigment particles in suspension on the wet block surface rather than allowing them to settle or run, and it controls the surface tension behavior of the ink on the block, affecting how it transfers to paper. Without nori, pigments print unevenly — grittily for minerals, over-fluidly for dilute organics.

Conventional concentration guidance:
- **Dense flat tone passes**: higher nori concentration (thicker paste) — holds pigment in place, produces opaque, even layer
- **Bokashi gradation passes**: lower nori concentration (watery paste) — allows the gradient to flow across the block surface, produces transparent gradation
- **Dilute glaze passes**: near-zero nori — pure pigment in water for maximum transparency

The dosa sizing agent (alum + animal hide glue) applied to washi before printing similarly affects how nori-pigment mixture penetrates the paper surface. Stronger dosa = pigment stays near surface = more intense, sharper color. Weaker dosa = pigment sinks into fiber = more diffuse, softer color. Standard concentration for making fresh dosa is 3–4% animal glue with 1% alum in warm water.

## Pigment Sequencing Rule

The light-to-dark sequencing rule in mokuhanga is not arbitrary — it is mechanically enforced by the physics of overprinting. Dark pigments applied over light ones produce a readable result; light pigments applied over dark ones vanish into the underlying color. Additionally, early light passes establish moisture in the paper that conditions it for later passes; later passes require less preliminary dampening.

The exception: sumi ink (black key block) in traditional ukiyo-e is applied last in some traditions, after all color blocks, because carbon black is the densest pigment and will override any underlying color if laid down first. Shibata's trained eye for when to apply each color is informed by decades of practice predicting how each pigment family behaves in wet overprint conditions.
